## Reel 9: How to Run Subject Line A/B Tests That Actually Teach You Something

**Why Now**: Most brands test subject lines randomly and learn nothing. There is a systematic methodology — test one variable at a time, build a running log — that consistently produces insight.

**Format**: Tactical walkthrough — screen recording with overlaid data

**Visual Notes**: Screen recording inside Klaviyo's campaign A/B test setup. Show the test configuration — two subject lines, split audience, minimum sample size. Show a running test log in a simple spreadsheet format. Show results from an actual test with the winning variant and what it tells you.

**Hook** (first 1-3 seconds):
> "Most brands A/B test subject lines and learn nothing. Because they're testing wrong. Here's the method that actually builds knowledge over time."

**Script**:
> "Most brands A/B test subject lines and walk away knowing nothing. They test 'Summer Sale' against 'Our Biggest Sale Yet' and discover one won by 3 percent. Then they forget about it and test something random next time.

> That's not testing. That's flipping a coin twice.

> [SHOW: Klaviyo A/B test setup — two subject line variants, audience split configuration]

> Here's the method that actually works. You test one variable category at a time, in sequence, across multiple sends.

> Round one: test the framing. Curiosity versus specificity. 'You're not going to believe this' versus '$30 off your next order, today only.' Run this across at least 1,000 recipients per variant — that's your minimum for statistical relevance.

> [SHOW: Test log in spreadsheet — showing category, variant A, variant B, winner, margin]

> Every result goes into a running log. Category tested. Variant A. Variant B. Winner. Margin. What you concluded.

> [SHOW: Round two framing — now testing length]

> Round two: length. Take your winning framing type and test short (4 words or fewer) versus medium (6 to 8 words). Repeat across multiple campaigns.

> [SHOW: Round three — personalization test]

> Round three: personalization. First name in subject line versus no personalization. Personalized subject lines lift opens by 26 percent on average — but that's an average. What does it do for your specific list?

> [SHOW: Round four — urgency testing]

> Round four: urgency. Hard deadline versus soft deadline versus no deadline at all.

> [SHOW: Accumulated test log with 8-10 rows of results]

> After three months of testing one thing at a time, you have something most brands never have: actual knowledge about what works for your audience. Not marketing advice. Your data.

> Start the log this week. Test one thing per campaign. The knowledge compounds."

**On-Screen Text/Caption**:
> "Subject line A/B testing — the method that actually teaches you something"

**Practical Takeaway**:
> Open a spreadsheet right now. Create columns: date, category tested, variant A, variant B, winner, margin, conclusion. Test your next campaign subject line as curiosity versus specificity, with at least 1,000 recipients per variant. Log the result. That's the beginning of real knowledge.

**CTA**:
> "Save this. What subject line variable have you never systematically tested? Drop it in the comments — I'll tell you how to set up the test."
